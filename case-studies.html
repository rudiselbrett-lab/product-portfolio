The Problem

AI adoption across the enterprise was fragmented. There was no centralized experience for interacting with models, guardrails for PII and compliance were inconsistent, and access to AI was limited across the organization. Teams were solving the same problems in isolation — or not solving them at all.

The Product

A unified AI platform providing a chat-based interface for employees, APIs for embedding AI into internal products, guardrails for safe and compliant usage, and multimodal and structured output capabilities. One coherent surface where any employee could interact with AI and any team could build on top of it.

My Role

I owned the platform roadmap and prioritization, defined core capabilities (chat, APIs, guardrails), and partnered across engineering, risk, and leadership to ship them. I used platform usage data — prompt volume, feature adoption, drop-off patterns — to continuously inform what to build next. Prioritization wasn't intuition; it was telemetry translated into roadmap decisions.

Why It Matters

Before Ally.ai, AI at the enterprise level was a capability available to a few technical teams. After it, AI was accessible and safe for everyday users — transforming how employees interact with data, tools, and workflows. The platform didn't just enable AI use cases; it made safe AI the default, not the exception.

The Problem

End users — PMs, ops teams, analysts — needed faster ways to extract insights from documents, help navigating complex internal processes, and assistance completing repetitive or knowledge-heavy tasks. Existing workflows were manual, time-intensive, and dependent on SMEs. The platform existed; the experiences that made it indispensable didn't yet.

The Product

A set of user-facing AI experiences embedded into Ally.ai: Document Q&A (upload a document, ask questions, get answers), structured summarization tools, an onboarding assistant that guides users through tools and workflows, and workflow copilots that help users complete tasks and retrieve answers in real time.

My Role

I identified the highest-value user workflows for AI augmentation — prioritized by frequency of task, time cost, and business impact. I defined the UX patterns for chat, document interaction, and copilots, then partnered with engineering to deliver experiences that were performant enough to trust and simple enough to actually use. The hardest part wasn't building the features; it was choosing which tasks were worth automating and which weren't.

What Users Can Do

Upload documents and query them instantly. Generate structured summaries without manual review. Ask questions about processes and get real-time guidance. Reduce reliance on SMEs for routine knowledge retrieval.

Why It Matters

This is where AI became real for users — not a platform in the abstract, but something they could touch, use, and rely on daily. The platform gave the organization the infrastructure; these experiences gave individual users a reason to show up.

The Problem

Operational workflows — especially in audit — were multi-step, manual, and difficult to scale. They depended on human review and coordination at each step, which created bottlenecks, introduced error, and made it impossible to increase throughput without increasing headcount.

The Product

A platform enabling agentic workflows where AI agents orchestrate tasks across systems, analyze documents and data, and automate multi-step processes end to end. Use cases included audit workflow automation, document review and summarization, and process orchestration across tools. The architecture was built for reuse — each workflow pattern could be applied across contexts, not just the one it was built for.

My Role

I defined the agent product strategy and use cases, built the reusable agent pattern library, and partnered with PwC's Tiger Team on Next Generation Audit — an initiative to apply agentic AI to the most complex workflows in external audit. The key product decision was to prioritize reusability over customization: every agent workflow we built had to be generalizable, or we weren't building it. That constraint is what made 10+ reusable patterns possible instead of 10 one-off builds.

Why It Matters

Chat-based AI augments humans. Agentic AI replaces whole steps in a process. This work extended AI from something that helps people do tasks faster into something that does tasks autonomously — unlocking step-function gains in efficiency that assistive tools can't reach. It also raised the stakes: autonomous systems require more rigorous evaluation, tighter scoping, and clearer fallback paths than any other AI product category.

The Problem

Teams struggled to discover available models, compare cost and performance, and safely onboard and deploy them. Every model evaluation was ad hoc. Every onboarding was a one-off. There was no shared understanding of what was available, what it cost, or how to get it into production — which meant teams either made uninformed choices or went back to engineering every time.

The Product

A model lifecycle and discovery system combining a model registry UI for discovery and comparison, registration workflows for onboarding, activation controls for deployment, and lifecycle visibility across environments. Teams could find the right model, understand the tradeoffs, and move it toward production without heavy technical dependency.

My Role

I defined the product requirements for discoverability — what metadata a team needs to make a model decision, and how to surface it without overwhelming them. I designed the onboarding and activation workflows and prioritized usability over completeness: a model registry that shows every possible attribute is a search engine. A good model registry shows the right attributes for the decision at hand. That distinction drove every design choice.

Why It Matters

This is the infrastructure layer that makes everything else sustainable. Without standardized model onboarding and lifecycle visibility, every new AI use case requires a custom engineering path to production. With it, teams can evaluate, onboard, and deploy models as a repeatable process — and the organization gets visibility into cost, performance, and risk across its entire model portfolio.
