Adoption Turnaround: Getting Teams to Actually Use an Enterprise AI Platform

Ally Financial · AI Product Manager · 2024–Present

9% → 21%
Adoption among eligible employees
9 → 43
Use cases in production
3x
More teams shipping without engineering

The Problem

The platform existed, but adoption was stuck around 9%. The capability wasn't the issue; the path to using it was. Teams couldn't get from idea to production without leaning on central engineering, so most either gave up or never started. A platform that's hard to adopt is, functionally, a platform nobody has.

The Product

A redesigned intake-to-production pipeline paired with a forward-deployed model, where the platform team embeds directly with a business line to build the first use case alongside them. Behavioral instrumentation underneath it all showed exactly where teams dropped off between discovery and shipping.

My Role

I treated adoption as a product problem, not a marketing one. I used product and behavioral data to find the specific drop-off points in the journey, then prioritized the friction that was actually costing us teams rather than the friction that was easiest to fix. Embedding with business lines taught me the difference between what we assumed teams needed and what they actually got stuck on, and that gap drove the roadmap.

The principle I kept coming back to: you don't grow adoption by promoting the platform, you grow it by removing the reasons people quit.

Why It Matters

Adoption is the only metric that proves a platform earns its place. Doubling it while tripling the number of teams who could ship independently is leverage: the platform got more valuable without the central team becoming the bottleneck for every new use case.

Safe by Default: Making AI Shippable in a Regulated Environment

Ally Financial · AI Product Manager · 2024–Present

100%
Platform traffic covered by guardrails and observability
10 / 4
Agents classified across four risk tiers
43
Use cases live on a governed foundation

The Problem

In a regulated bank, AI can't reach production without controls. But controls are usually bolted on at the end, which either slows teams to a crawl or pushes them to avoid shipping entirely. Every team was reinventing its own guardrails or quietly steering clear of AI altogether. There was no shared, trusted foundation to build on.

The Product

A governed foundation that teams inherit instead of rebuild: enterprise guardrails, an LLM observability layer covering 100% of platform traffic, and an agent classification framework that sorts agents by privilege, data sensitivity, and ownership — so the right level of control is applied automatically. Safety became part of the platform rather than a checkpoint at the end of it.

My Role

I defined what "safe by default" needed to look like from the builder's point of view, then partnered closely with the enterprise risk and model risk teams — working within their frameworks rather than around them — to make the compliant path the path of least resistance.

The design goal was specific: a team should get safety for free by building on the platform, not earn it by surviving a review. The principle underneath it: governance done well is what lets you move fast, because the safe option is also the easy one.

Why It Matters

Trust is the precondition for scale. You can't put AI in front of real users in a regulated setting until the controls are real, and you can't ship dozens of use cases if every one needs a custom safety review. Making safety infrastructure rather than a gate is what let the portfolio grow without the risk growing with it.

Agentic AI Zero-to-One PricewaterhouseCoopers

Agentic AI for Audit: A Zero-to-One Product in Six Weeks

PricewaterhouseCoopers · Associate Product Manager · 2023–2024

6 weeks
Concept to working MVP
1,000
Auditors in the pilot
120,000+
Practitioners on the platform it was adopted into

The Problem

Auditors spent enormous amounts of time manually reading through documents to surface findings worth a human's attention. It was slow, inconsistent across people, and impossible to scale as document volume grew. The judgment was genuinely human work — but the reading-and-flagging in front of it was a bottleneck.

The Product

An agentic system that ingested and parsed documents, surfaced the likely findings, and escalated them to a human for review rather than deciding on its own. The machine did the exhaustive first pass; the auditor made the call. The product was really the handoff: getting the human-in-the-loop escalation right so people trusted what surfaced and knew what still needed their eyes.

My Role

I worked as part of a 20-person pilot team that took this from concept to a working MVP in six weeks. My contribution was on the product side, helping turn an ambiguous, emerging capability into something auditors could actually use and trust.

The lesson that stuck with me: the win wasn't automating the auditor away, it was designing the seam between machine and human so the machine earned trust one good handoff at a time.

Why It Matters

This is zero-to-one AI product work under real ambiguity and a hard timeline: no clean spec, a brand-new capability, and a six-week clock. That it was piloted with 1,000 auditors and then folded into the next-generation platform serving a global practice of 120,000+ is the proof it generalized beyond a demo.
