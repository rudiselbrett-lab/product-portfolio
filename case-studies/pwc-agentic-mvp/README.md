# Agentic AI MVP — PricewaterhouseCoopers

**Company:** PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC)  
**Role:** Associate Product Manager  
**Team:** 20-person pilot team (product, engineering, business stakeholders)  
**Timeline:** 6-week delivery sprint (within Jun 2023–Nov 2024 tenure)

---

## Context

PwC was exploring how agentic AI could be applied to an internal workflow. This wasn't a research project — the goal was a working MVP in six weeks with a real pilot team using it. The capability was emerging (this was early in the agentic AI wave) and the problem definition was deliberately open: we had a hypothesis, not a spec.

---

## The challenge

Six weeks is a brutal timeline for anything involving LLMs and multi-step agent workflows. The failure modes are obvious: over-scoping, chasing capability instead of use case, or building something technically impressive that nobody actually uses.

The first thing I did was narrow the problem.

Rather than trying to demonstrate everything agentic AI *could* do, I pushed the team to answer: what is the one workflow where an agent that can reason, retrieve, and act would make a material difference to a real user in six weeks? That question forced us to choose.

---

## What I contributed

**Problem definition and scoping.** Worked with senior product leadership and business stakeholders to define a specific, bounded use case that was ambitious enough to demonstrate the capability but constrained enough to ship in six weeks. The scoping conversation was as important as any technical decision.

**Success metrics and OKRs.** Defined what success looked like before a line of code was written. Alignment on success criteria at the start prevented scope creep and gave the team a clear signal for when to stop building and start testing.

**Backlog and stakeholder management.** With a 20-person team and a six-week clock, backlog discipline mattered. I translated ambiguous business needs into prioritized requirements, ran sprint ceremonies, and kept the team focused on the critical path.

**Cross-functional coordination.** Engineering, product, and business stakeholders had different definitions of "done." My job was to hold those together without letting the gaps slow us down.

---

## What we delivered

A working agentic AI MVP with a live pilot team — from a fuzzy capability hypothesis to a deployed prototype in six weeks. The MVP demonstrated the core agentic loop: the agent could reason about a problem, retrieve relevant context, and take a structured action without human intervention at each step.

The pilot team used it. That was the bar.

---

## What this taught me

**Agentic AI products need tighter constraints than regular software, not looser ones.** When the system can reason and act autonomously, the surface area for unexpected behavior is much larger. Scoping tightly isn't timidity — it's how you get something trustworthy into users' hands fast.

**"Working MVP" and "impressive demo" are different things.** The goal was a pilot team using the system to do real work, not a polished demo for executives. Keeping those goals distinct shaped every prioritization decision.

**OKRs at the start of an AI project are unusually high-leverage.** Because the technology is new and the outcomes are uncertain, having agreed-upon success criteria before you start prevents the goalpost from moving when you hit the inevitable unexpected results.
