Philosophy

How I think about product work

The beliefs, principles, and operating patterns I return to — especially when things get complicated.

Did a real person's life get easier, and can you prove they're using what you built?

Everything else — the specs, the ceremonies, the roadmaps — is in service of that or it's waste. Too many teams measure themselves by features shipped instead of problems solved. My job is to resist that drift.

01

Start with the people who have the problem

I lean toward a forward-deployed model: embed with the business, run discovery alongside them, and turn operational pain into a clear opportunity before anyone writes code. I don't wait for tidy requirements to land on my desk, because they're usually wrong when they do.

Finding the right problem is the hard part. Once it's understood, building is the easy part.

02

How I work: a simple loop

ListenBefore assuming I know the answer
OrientAround where the real leverage is
Own a WedgePick a narrow, winnable first slice instead of solving everything at once
PushInto production — nothing counts until it ships
MeasureWhat happened and feed it back

The discipline isn't knowing the steps. It's refusing to skip Listen and Orient when I'm sure I already have the answer — which is exactly when I'm most likely to be wrong.

03

How I prioritize: sequence, don't score

I use Weighted Shortest Job First (WSJF) — weighing the value and urgency of a piece of work against the effort to deliver it, and doing the high-value, low-effort work first. It keeps me honest about cost of delay and stops the roadmap from filling up with expensive bets that keep getting deferred.

04

How I compose a release: Proven, Better, New

Proven
A known win I'm confident in
Better
A clear improvement on something that already exists
New
An experimental bet worth taking even if it fails

A roadmap built only on proven wins goes stale. One built only on new bets is reckless. The balance is the point.

05

With AI: evals over specs

I'd rather define how we'll know it's working than write a long document nobody opens after sprint two. A spec describes intent; an eval describes reality. I define the test for "good" up front, in measurable terms, and build toward passing it.

That matters more as systems get less deterministic. The only way to trust an AI product in production is to decide in advance how you'd catch it failing.

06

On platforms: own the control plane, rent the execution plane

The durable advantage is in owning the governance, routing, evaluation, and standards that make many use cases safe and reusable — not in rebuilding what a vendor API already does well.

The tension I keep returning to is depth versus breadth. A bank is almost always better served by one or two deep, reusable capabilities than by a long tail of shallow pilots that duplicate each other. Saying no to those pilots is some of the most important work I do.

07

Governance is a feature, not a tax

In a regulated environment, controls done well are what let you move fast. The teams that get this ship more, not less — because they've made the safe path the easy path.

08

What I refuse to do

  • Build for the roadmap's sake
  • Chase novelty with no route to adoption
  • Let process exist for its own sake
