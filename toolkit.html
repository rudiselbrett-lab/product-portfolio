PM Toolkit

How I think, decide, and deliver

The frameworks, mental models, and operating principles I use across discovery, prioritization, AI governance, and delivery.

Discovery & Opportunity Assessment

Forward-Deployed Discovery

Before writing any requirements, I embed directly with users and business line teams to understand their real operational problems. The question I lead with: what are you doing manually today that you shouldn't be?

This approach consistently surfaces higher-value problems than stakeholder interviews alone — and builds the trust that makes adoption easier later.

Problem vs. Solution Separation

Clients and stakeholders arrive with solutions, not problems. My discovery process works backwards: what outcome do they need, what's preventing it today, and is the proposed solution actually the right one?

At Terazo, this pivot — from "we need a dashboard" to "we need push notifications at decision points" — happened before development started and saved months of rework.

Discovery in Regulated Environments

Standard discovery assumes fast prototyping. In financial services and healthcare, data access for research is constrained and compliance is a first-class constraint.

I bring risk and compliance stakeholders into problem framing — not just solution review — so the product direction is viable before development starts.

AI Product Strategy

Evals Before Build

For any AI feature, I define what "good" looks like before engineering starts. What does success mean to a user vs. to a metric? Where do the failure modes live, and how do we weight them?

Getting alignment on those questions early prevents the expensive pivots that happen when a model ships and nobody agrees on whether it's working.

Tight Scoping for Agentic Systems

Agentic AI products need tighter constraints than regular software, not looser ones. When a system can reason and act autonomously, the surface area for unexpected behavior is large.

I scope agentic work to a single, bounded workflow where the agent's actions are auditable and the fallback for uncertainty is clear.

Reusable Implementation Patterns

One-off AI builds don't scale. I identify common use case types (summarization, Q&A, workflow automation, structured extraction) and systematize their delivery into reusable patterns.

At Ally Financial, this cut onboarding time by ~50% and enabled 3x more teams to ship with minimal engineering support.

Portfolio Prioritization

Intake Screening

Every incoming opportunity gets a structured intake: what's the problem, who's affected, who owns the outcome, and what does better look like in measurable terms? An idea without a business line sponsor and a measurable outcome doesn't proceed.

Four-Dimension Scoring

I score opportunities on business value, strategic alignment, feasibility, and risk profile. This separates the "sounds cool" ideas from the ones worth investing in — and produces a prioritization that's defensible to stakeholders who didn't make the cut.

Concentration Over Coverage

More AI use cases isn't better. I apply an 80/20 lens to portfolios: identify the 13 things that represent 80% of the value and protect them from being crowded out by the long tail of acceptable-but-not-great ideas.

At Ally, screening out ~30% of low-value initiatives is what made the high-value ones possible.

AI Governance

Governance as Infrastructure

The wrong mental model: governance is a checklist you complete at the end to get approval to ship. The right model: governance is infrastructure built into the product and delivery process.

Safety controls, content filters, and PII protection are product requirements with acceptance criteria — tested in QA, not added post-launch.

LLM Observability as a Data Product

Every prompt and response on the platform should be logged, tagged, and queryable. This serves risk teams (audit capability) and product teams (prompt quality, failure mode identification) simultaneously.

MRM Review as a Delivery Milestone

In financial services, Model Risk Management review is required for certain AI use cases. I map the MRM path at intake and build required documentation into the sprint process.

When MRM review is predictable, it's fast. When it's ad hoc, it's slow and expensive.

Delivery & Adoption

Adoption by Design

I design for adoption from day one — not as a post-launch concern. This means embedding feedback loops in the product, identifying change management requirements during discovery, and setting success metrics that reflect behavioral change, not feature availability.

OKRs at the Start

I define success metrics before engineering starts, especially for AI products where outcomes are uncertain. Agreed-upon success criteria prevent the goalpost from moving when unexpected results appear — which they always do.

Decision Docs

I write down the tradeoffs on non-obvious decisions. When a choice is reversible, I move fast. When it's not, I slow down and make the reasoning legible — so future teams understand why, not just what.
